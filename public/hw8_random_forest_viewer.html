Bike Rental Demand with Random Forest Regression

Seoul's public bike-sharing system records hourly rental counts alongside weather and calendar features: temperature, humidity, rainfall, snowfall, solar radiation, hour, season, and holiday flags. The question is whether a regression model can predict demand from those signals. I trained two models on the same train/validation/test split: ordinary linear regression and a random forest of 100 trees. Linear regression scores R² = 0.54 on the test set, only about half the variance explained. The same pipeline with random forest jumps to R² = 0.86. The reason sits in the features themselves. Most of them are not linearly related to bike count. Demand peaks twice a day around commute hours, follows a temperature sweet spot rather than rising forever, and responds in step-function fashion to rain and to functioning vs non-functioning days. A single line through the data can't bend that way. An ensemble of trees can.

Try it

The 1D playground below makes the gap visible. Pick a feature pattern and the demo trains both models live on noisy samples drawn from that ground truth. The dashed gray line is the true relationship; the purple line is the linear fit; the teal curve is the random forest's averaged prediction. On the linear preset the two models almost agree. On the others, the line stays flat-footed while the ensemble traces the bends. Slide the trees count to see how a small forest already smooths out a noisy single tree, and slide max depth to watch the forest gain or lose the ability to capture sharp peaks.
