Overall, the decision tree model is highly accurate, regardless of whether impurity is measured using gini or entropy. A clear trend is that the models become more accurate as additional levels are added to the tree. Initially, at level 1, the accuracy is 80%, with poor precision for identifying benign URLs (51%, as mentioned earlier). By level 2, accuracy increases to 86%. Interestingly, the classification of benign URLs remains unchanged (the counts of true positives and false negatives are the same), but precision improves by 11% because fewer malicious URLs are incorrectly classified as benign. At levels 1 and 2, both gini and entropy yield similar results, but this shifts at level 3. Here, the gini based model achieves 90% accuracy, outperforming the entropy-based model at 86%. This difference could be due to gini’s simpler, more decisive splits that tend to generalize better as tree depth increases. Both models continue to improve through levels 4 and 5, with the gini based model maintaining a slight edge in accuracy. Finally, at level 6, accuracy reaches 93% for both models. Less than 30 benign URLs are misclassified in each model, but there's still room for improvement, as precision for classifying benign URLs remains around 75-77%. For that metric, the entropy model struggles slightly more, with 509 malicious URLs misclassified as benign, compared to 460 in the gini based model.

The root node has a gini index of 0.5, which indicates maximum impurity—this means there’s an equal number of benign and malicious URLs at this level. This is expected because the training set was undersampled to balance the classes and improve accuracy. At the root, the tree uses SymbolCount_Domain, which represents the number of special characters in the URL. A higher count often indicates a suspicious or malicious URL, as attackers might use symbols to mimic real URLs (e.g., "F@ce_book.com"). The tree finds that URLs with 1.5 or fewer special characters form a good cutoff, splitting the data into two groups: 7,193 URLs on the left and 5,257 on the right. The left child node has a gini index of 0.327, which is an improvement but still shows some impurity. It then uses urlLen (URL length) as a splitting criterion. URLs shorter than 51 characters are classified as non-benign (malicious), with only 2 misclassified out of 488, resulting in a very low gini index of 0.008—indicating near-perfect classification. Longer URLs (51 characters or more) are mostly benign (85%), but 996 out of 6,705 are misclassified, leading to a gini of 0.253. Back at the first split, URLs with more than 1.5 special characters go to the right node, which uses delimeter_path (number of delimiters in the URL) as a criterion. This node already has a relatively low gini of 0.176, with a 514-4,743 split. If a URL has fewer than 6 delimiters, the gini drops further to 0.105, reducing misclassifications to 262. However, if a URL has 6 or more delimiters, the classification becomes uncertain again, with a nearly even split and a gini of 0.498. However, this leaf only has 541 instances out of the original 12,450.
